This can be seen in the following concept map.</p><p><a href="http://localhost:8000/rep.test-content/packages/ele_1301_s1/media/resources/cmap/comb_logic_design.html">Combinational logic (concept map)</a></p><h1><a id="id3" name="id3"><!--id3--></a>Objectives</h1><p class="P5">At the completion of this module you will be able to:</p><ul class="lib"><li><p>demonstrate a working knowledge of Boolean algebra by minimising logic equations</p></li><li><p>understand mapping techniques and their use in minimising logic equations</p></li><li><p>identify <span class="spCh spChx2018">&#x2018;</span><b>Don<span class="spCh spChx2019">&#x2019;</span>t Care</b><span class="spCh spChx2019">&#x2019;</span> conditions and use them to minimise logic equations.<br/></p></li></ul><h1><a id="id5" name="id5"><!--id5--></a>3.1 Boolean algebra</h1><p>In the mid 1800<span class="spCh spChx2019">&#x2019;</span>s, a mathematical proposition was presented which simplified and allowed manipulation of algebraic expressions. The presenter was an English mathematician named George <b>Boole</b> and the technique he developed was known as <b>Boolean Algebra</b>.</p><p>Its application to two-state (binary) switching circuits was proposed in 1938 by Claude <b>Shannon</b> at Massachusetts Institute of Technology. The terms <span class="spCh spChx2018">&#x2018;</span>AND gate<span class="spCh spChx2019">&#x2019;</span> and <span class="spCh spChx2018">&#x2018;</span>OR gate<span class="spCh spChx2019">&#x2019;</span> originated from Shannon<span class="spCh spChx2019">&#x2019;</span>s paper.</p><p>Boolean algebra provides a straight forward approach to the design and simplification of electronic switching systems. This of course leads to a reduction in the total number of gates required to implement a logical operation which in turn makes the final design more reliable, less complicated and therefore less expensive to produce.</p><p>A Boolean <b>variable</b> (A) can have two possible values which can be indicated by a 0 or a 1.</p><p>i.e.    A can equal 0 in some cases and A can equal 1 in others.</p><p>A logic designer would always specify which of the above 2 conditions is being used for a particular design.</p><p>The Boolean inverse of A is <a name="Object1"><!-- --></a><img alt="Object1" class="fr6" height="21" src="module03_files/587f9621_17x21bw.png" style="border:0px; vertical-align: middle" width="17"/><span class="T1">. </span>The inverse of C is <a name="Object2"><!-- --></a><img alt="Object2" class="fr7" height="22" src="module03_files/m59d40452_15x22bw.png" style="border:0px; vertical-align: middle" width="15"/> etc.</p><p>A Boolean <b>function</b> is an algebraic expression containing the variables in terms appropriate to a particular design. The variables may appear in the terms in either <b>true</b> (A) or <b>complement</b> (<a name="Object3"><!-- --></a><img alt="Object3" class="fr6" height="20" src="module03_files/m77f604e6_16x20bw.png" style="border:0px; vertical-align: middle" width="16"/>) form.</p><p>The terms may appear in the function (or algebraic expression) in either the <span class="spCh spChx2018">&#x2018;</span>product<span class="spCh spChx2019">&#x2019;</span> form (ABC) or the <span class="spCh spChx2018">&#x2018;</span>sum<span class="spCh spChx2019">&#x2019;</span> form (A+B+C). Here of course the <span class="spCh spChx2018">&#x2018;</span>product<span class="spCh spChx2019">&#x2019;</span> form means logical AND and the <span class="spCh spChx2018">&#x2018;</span>sum<span class="spCh spChx2019">&#x2019;</span> form means logical OR. No other mathematical symbols are used in Boolean algebra.</p><p class="P8">Two basic forms of Boolean functions are of special interest because of their common occurrence and symmetrical form.</p><p class="P8">These are called <b>canonical</b> forms. They are:</p><ul class="lib"><li><p>the product of sums (called <b>maxterm</b> form)</p></li><li><p>the sum of products (called <b>minterm</b> form).<br/></p></li></ul><p>In order for either form to be canonical, <b>all</b> variables involved must appear in <b>all</b> terms of the expression in either true or complement form.</p><p>A <b>minterm</b> for three variables A, B and C is a <b>product</b> form such as: <a name="Object32"><!-- --></a><img alt="Object32" class="fr8" height="21" src="module03_files/4f81d8f0_108x21bw.png" style="border:0px; vertical-align: middle" width="108"/></p><p>A <b>maxterm</b> for the same three variables is a <b>sum</b> form such as: <a name="Object4"><!-- --></a><img alt="Object4" class="fr9" height="21" src="module03_files/m5ea9aeeb_172x21bw.png" style="border:0px; vertical-align: middle" width="172"/></p><p>For three variables there are eight possible combinations or terms.
