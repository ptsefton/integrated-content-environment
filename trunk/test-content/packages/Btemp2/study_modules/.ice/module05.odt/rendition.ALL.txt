span.T13 {font-size: smaller; font-size:11pt; vertical-align: sub; }</style></head><body><h1><a id="id2" name="id2"><!--id2--></a>Overview</h1><p>Combinational logic systems, no matter how complex, produce outputs that are strict functions of the inputs. Flip-flops, counters and shift registers, on the other hand, respond not only to the inputs but also the current state of the device. That is, the system has memory. So the output is dependent on the 'state' of the system.  These systems are classified as sequential logic systems, as shown in the following concept map.</p><p><a href="http://localhost:8000/rep.test-content/packages/ele_1301_s1/media/resources/cmap/sequ_logic_sys.html">Sequential logic systems (concept map)</a></p><h1><a id="id3" name="id3"><!--id3--></a>Objectives</h1><p class="P9">At the completion of this module you will be able to:</p><ul class="lib"><li><p>describe the use of basic logic gates in developing the set/reset (SR) bistable or flip flop</p></li><li><p>explain the use of graphical timing diagrams to describe the operation of basic logic systems</p></li><li><p>recognise and describe the operation of typical flip flops including D, MS, T and JK systems</p></li><li><p>outline the principles of digital counters</p></li><li><p>develop and design typical serial and parallel counting systems</p></li><li><p>list the problems associated with propagation delay and stability in counting systems</p></li><li><p>describe the principles of feedback in counting systems and its use in shift register development.<br/></p></li></ul><h1><a id="id5" name="id5"><!--id5--></a>5.1 The bistable or flip flop</h1><p>In counting and sequential networks it is necessary to provide some form of <b>memory element</b> to record the state of a problem at any moment in time.</p><p>The most commonly used device is known as the <b>flip flop</b> or <b>bistable</b> element as it has 2&#xA0;stable states. These correspond to logic levels 0 and 1, the output of the element changing from one state to the other on demand.</p><p>A flip flop therefore may be defined as a device which stores binary information in the form of a 0 or a 1 state and can be maintained indefinitely in either of those states provided it is continually supplied with power. It can also be easily changed from one state to the other.</p><p>There are a large number of different types of flip flop. The flip flops most commonly used have names like: SR flip flop, (set-reset); D flip flop; T flip flop and JK flip flop. We will look at the characteristics of these flip flops.</p><h1><a id="id6" name="id6"><!--id6--></a>5.2 General principles of flip flops</h1><p>A block diagram of a typical flip flop is shown:</p><p><a name="graphics2"><!-- --></a><img alt="graphics2" class="fr1" height="122" src="module05_files/28ebcea2_340x122.jpg" style="border:0px; vertical-align: top" width="340"/></p><p>The input signals are combined in the input logic network to give one or more outputs to the flip flop. There may also be another input to the FF known as a <b>clock pulse</b>. This is sometimes used to ensure the operation of the FF occurs at a particular time. The outputs from the FF are Q and its complement <a name="Object12"><!-- --></a><img alt="Object12" class="fr5" height="24" src="module05_files/28251b81_16x24bw.png" style="border:0px; vertical-align: middle" width="16"/>.</p><p>Where a clock pulse is <b>not</b> used, the system is said to be <b>asynchronous</b> since the input to the FF may be dependant on the output of some previous network or even flip flop. Where a clock pulse is used, the system is usually called <b>synchronous</b>.</p><h1><a id="id7" name="id7"><!--id7--></a>5.3 The basic bistable construction</h1><p>A basic bistable could be constructed from two inverters as shown:</p><p><a name="graphics3"><!-- --></a><img alt="graphics3" class="fr1" height="98" src="module05_files/m4b009b4c_227x98.jpg" style="border:0px; That is, it stores a logic 1 at its output. In many applications a stream of data may be being generated during a process, but its desirable to only store the final result.</p><p>A typical example would be in computer multiplication or a digital counter where only the final result is needed, <b>not</b> all the intermediate steps.</p><p>The storage element then is <b>isolated</b> from the output during the calculating phase and finally <b>enabled</b> by a timing pulse at the end of calculations in time to receive the result.</p><p>This timing pulse is often called a <b><span class="spCh spChx2018">&#x2018;</span>clock pulse<span class="spCh spChx2019">&#x2019;</span></b> or <b><span class="spCh spChx2018">&#x2018;</span>strobe<span class="spCh spChx2019">&#x2019;</span></b> or <b><span class="spCh spChx2018">&#x2018;</span>gate pulse<span class="spCh spChx2019">&#x2019;</span></b>. It is usually a pulse of short duration (in time) as shown in the diagram. We can draw a <b>clocked SR-NAND flip flop</b>.</p><p class="indent"><a name="graphics9"><!-- --></a><img alt="graphics9" class="fr1" height="303" src="module05_files/6083c688_463x303.jpg" style="border:0px; vertical-align: top" width="463"/></p><p>To study its operation using a timing diagram,</p><p>Let   Q  =  0,   S  =  1,   R  =  0,   CK  =  0</p><div class="Table8" style="width: 100%; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; text-align:left;"><table class="Table8" style="border-spacing: 0;empty-cells: show; width:14.601cm; border-collapse: collapse; "><colgroup><col style="width:1.485cm;"/><col style="width:13.116cm;"/></colgroup><tbody><tr><td style="vertical-align: top; "><p><b>Note:</b></p></td><td style="vertical-align: top; "><p>The necessary set or reset conditions are established on gates <span class="T4"><span class="spCh spChx2460">&#x2460;</span></span> and <span class="T4"><span class="spCh spChx2461">&#x2461;</span></span> <span style="text-decoration:underline; "><span class="underline"><b>but</b></span></span> are only transmitted to gates and on receipt of the clock pulse. The clock pulse is said to enable gates <span class="T4"><span class="spCh spChx2460">&#x2460;</span></span> and <span class="T4"><span class="spCh spChx2461">&#x2461;</span>.</span></p></td></tr></tbody></table></div><p/><p class="indent"><a name="graphics49"><!-- --></a><img alt="graphics49" class="fr1" height="408" src="module05_files/33dd271b_500x408.jpg" style="border:0px; vertical-align: top" width="500"/></p><p>When the clock is applied at t = 1, the FF will set and Q = 1.</p><p>At t = 2, the set line is returned to zero and no change will result at t = 3, the reset line is set to a 1. Again nothing can happen until the clock is present as well. This is known as the <span class="spCh spChx2018">&#x2018;</span>ready to be reset<span class="spCh spChx2019">&#x2019;</span> condition.</p><p>At t = 5 the reset line can be set to a zero with no change.</p><h2><a id="id10" name="id10"><!--id10--></a>5.4.1 The <span class="spCh spChx2018">&#x2018;</span>D<span class="spCh spChx2019">&#x2019;</span> flip flop</h2><p>So far the flip flops we have considered require at least 2 inputs, S and R. This is called <b>double-rail</b> input data. Should S and R equal each other we have a 'not allowed' condition.</p><p>If an inverter is placed between S and R we could prevent this condition ever occurring.</p><p>Further only a single line of input data is needed to operate the flip flop. This is called <b>single-rail</b> data.</p><p>A flip flop constructed in this fashion is called a <b>D latch</b> or <b>D flip flop</b>.</p><p>Using a clocked-NAND SR flip flop we have:</p><p class="indent"><a name="graphics10"><!-- --></a><img alt="graphics10" class="fr1" height="125" src="module05_files/564503d1_466x125.jpg" style="border:0px; vertical-align: top" width="466"/></p><p class="indent"><a name="graphics11"><!-- --></a><img alt="graphics11" class="fr1" height="294" src="module05_files/490ac59d_472x294.jpg" style="border:0px; vertical-align: top" width="472"/></p><h2><a id="id11" name="id11"><!--id11--></a>5.4.2 The Master-Slave (MS) flip flop</h2><p>In the SR flip flop, there is sometimes inadequate isolation between <b>input</b> and <b>output</b>.</p><p>Complete isolation can be obtained by cascading 2 SR flip flops known as a <b>master</b> and a <b>slave</b> and clocking the two out of phase.</p><p>This is called a master-slave (M-S) flip flop.</p><p class="indent"><a name="graphics12"><!-- --></a><img alt="graphics12" class="fr1" height="168" src="module05_files/m47006a38_523x168.jpg" style="border:0px; vertical-align: top" width="523"/></p><p>In operation, when the clock is <b>low</b>, the master is disabled and the slave is enabled and vice versa. This is due to the inverter being placed in the clock input circuit to the slave FF.</p><p class="indent"><a name="graphics13"><!-- --></a><img alt="graphics13" class="fr1" height="125" src="module05_files/16bf1030_430x125.jpg" style="border:0px; vertical-align: top" width="430"/></p><p>So during the time when the clock pulse is <b>low</b> the output conditions of the master flip flop are transferred to the slave which responds accordingly. When the clock is <b>high</b> the master is isolated from the slave and the master can respond to its new inputs.</p><p>You can see that with correct timing of the clock only the desired signals may be accepted by the master and transferred to the slave outputs.  Another way to interpret this is to say the Master responds to the rising edge of the input clock pulse and the Slave responds to the falling edge of the input clock pulse or the output of the master-slave flip flop changes on the falling edge of the input clock pulse.</p><h2><a id="id12" name="id12"><!--id12--></a>5.4.3 The <span class="spCh spChx2018">&#x2018;</span>T<span class="spCh spChx2019">&#x2019;</span> flip flop</h2><p>If we now apply feedback to a master-slave flip flop and feed the slave outputs cross-connected back to the master inputs we create a system which changes state on the falling edge of each clock pulse. By changing state on each clock pulse, we say the flip flop <span class="spCh spChx2018">&#x2018;</span>toggles<span class="spCh spChx2019">&#x2019;</span> on each clock. This is why it is known as a <span class="spCh spChx2018">&#x2018;</span>T<span class="spCh spChx2019">&#x2019;</span> flip flop.</p><p class="indent"><a name="graphics14"><!-- --></a><img alt="graphics14" class="fr2" height="260" src="module05_files/9e43742_519x260.jpg" style="border:0px; vertical-align: top" width="519"/></p><p>The timing diagram is:</p><p><a name="graphics15"><!-- --></a><img alt="graphics15" class="fr1" height="301" src="module05_files/m167fd1d8_486x301.jpg" style="border:0px; vertical-align: top" width="486"/></p><h2><a id="id13" name="id13"><!--id13--></a>5.4.4 The J-K flip flop</h2><p>If we now use a clocked SR master slave flip flop with 3-input NAND gates for the master, we create the J-K flip flop.</p><p><a name="graphics16"><!-- --></a><img alt="graphics16" class="fr2" height="357" src="module05_files/63d800f_538x357.jpg" style="border:0px; vertical-align: top" width="538"/></p><p>In operation it behaves exactly like an SR FF where J equals the S input and K equals the R input. Counters are used in generating timing sequences to control precise operations such as clock pulses. They may be used in counting the number of times a particular event occurs in a system.</p><p>A counter is a logic device which counts through a prescribed sequence of states upon the application of some input pulses. The input pulses may arrive from some other external device or they may be clock pulses arriving regularly or at random.</p><p>Counters may be asynchronous, simply counting whenever input pulses are received or they may be synchronous meaning their entire counting operation can only occur when a clock pulse is received as well as the input pulses to be counted.</p><p>The flip flop is usually used as the basic building block for counter construction. The flip flops may be cascaded serially in which case the operation of any one flip flop depends upon the operation of its predecessor, or they may be connected in parallel in which case all flip flops change state simultaneously usually on receipt of a clock pulse.</p><p>One of the simplest forms of counting is the binary sequence counter.</p><p>An N-bit binary counter consists of N flip flops and can count from zero to 2<sup>N</sup><span class="spCh spChx2013">&#x2013;</span>1.</p><h2><a id="id16" name="id16"><!--id16--></a>5.5.1 Serial counting</h2><p>Let us examine a logic system which is known as a serial counter or <span class="spCh spChx2018">&#x2018;</span>ripple through<span class="spCh spChx2019">&#x2019;</span> counter. In this, the input to be counted is applied to the first flip flop and its output is connected to the next flip flop etc. The diagram shows a basic 4 bit counter constructed by T-flip flops.</p><p><span style="display: block"><a name="graphics17"><!-- --></a><img alt="graphics17" class="fr3" height="222" src="module05_files/47968428_529x222.jpg" style="border:0px; vertical-align: top" width="529"/></span>We know from section 5.4 that a T flip flop changes state on one-to-zero transitions applied to its T input.</p><p>Let us now consider the code table for a binary count from zero to fifteen.</p><p><a name="graphics18"><!-- --></a><img alt="graphics18" class="fr1" height="384" src="module05_files/346779a5_549x384.jpg" style="border:0px; vertical-align: top" width="549"/></p><h2><a id="id17" name="id17"><!--id17--></a>5.5.2 Propagation delay</h2><p>In practice, due to the time taken for electronic signals to travel through the circuitry, each flip flop has a time delay between the input signal and its output change. This is called the <b>propagation delay</b> of the device and is usually quite small in individual electronic devices (&lt; 1 <span class="T3"><span class="spCh spChxb5">&#xB5;</span></span> S) but can be of the order of milliseconds for whole systems. Problems could occur because of this delay.</p><p>Consider a binary counter as it counts from decimal 3 (0011) to decimal 4 (0100).</p><p>Assume that each flip flop has a propagation delay of <a name="Object4"><!-- --></a><img alt="Object4" class="fr10" height="13" src="module05_files/707e823a_12x13bw.png" style="border:0px; vertical-align: middle" width="12"/> <span class="T7">seconds. </span>If we observe a timing diagram as our counter goes from 3<sub>10</sub> to 4<sub>10</sub> we have:</p><p><span style="display: block"><a name="graphics19"><!-- --></a><img alt="graphics19" class="fr4" height="399" src="module05_files/m4096add4_529x399.jpg" style="border:0px; vertical-align: baseline; margin-bottom: 0.00345010615711px;" width="529"/></span></p><p>The system finally gets from 3 to 4 after taking <a name="Object2"><!-- --></a><img alt="Object2" class="fr11" height="17" src="module05_files/m68d52398_19x17bw.png" style="border:0px; vertical-align: middle" width="19"/> <span class="T7">seconds</span> (3 transient counts) to do so <b>but</b> notice that it also had 2 transient counts of 2<sub>10</sub> and 0 on the way! Sometimes these errors or transient states don<span class="spCh spChx2019">&#x2019;</span>t matter, sometimes they do. We note that each stage, except the least significant stage, changes state when the previous stage changes from 0 to 1. The least significant stage changes state for each input pulse.</p><p>For example, if the state of the counter is 0000 at some time, then the next input pulse must set it to 1111 and the next to 1110, to 1101 etc.</p><p>Using T flip flops, reverse counting may be obtained by connecting an inverter in the input of each next stage.</p><h2><a id="id19" name="id19"><!--id19--></a>5.5.4 Non-pure binary counting</h2><p>In the counters we have considered so far the output of all the flip flops is zero simultaneously on a count of 0000 and again after the 16th count, 32nd count and so on.</p><p>Suppose the counter needs to be reset to zero after some value e.g. 5 or 10 etc., extra logic elements can be used to effect this. The application of a <span class="spCh spChx2018">&#x2018;</span>shift pulse<span class="spCh spChx2019">&#x2019;</span> moves the whole pattern one step along the register.</p><p>Consider the flip flops connected as shown:</p><p class="indent"><a name="graphics29"><!-- --></a><img alt="graphics29" class="fr1" height="227" src="module05_files/m33e2b1a2_495x227.jpg" style="border:0px; vertical-align: top" width="495"/></p><p>Assume all stages except FFC are initially set to zero. These logic states are marked on the diagram. When the first shift pulse arrives, FFC is reset, FFB is set, FFA is unchanged. The next shift pulse sets FFA, resets FFB and leaves FFC unchanged. You can see that the logic 1 originally in FFC has progressively moved through the register. Continual shift pulses move the pattern around and around as though it were a circular device or ring.</p><p>For this reason these devices are sometimes called <span class="spCh spChx2018">&#x2018;</span>ring counters<span class="spCh spChx2019">&#x2019;</span>. The number of times a given pattern is repeated is determined by the initial code set into the register.</p><p>Let us consider the pattern generated by this shift register.</p><p class="indent"><a name="graphics30"><!-- --></a><img alt="graphics30" class="fr1" height="142" src="module05_files/m1fc0bb46_487x142.jpg" style="border:0px; vertical-align: top" width="487"/></p><h2><a id="id26" name="id26"><!--id26--></a>5.6.1 Feedback variations</h2><p>By feeding back various functions of the state of the register to its input, shift registers can be made to generate a sequence of binary combinations which need not necessarily follow conventional sequences. For instance by feeding back the <b><span class="spCh spChx2018">&#x2018;</span>complement<span class="spCh spChx2019">&#x2019;</span></b> of the last stage we can generate a pattern of length 2N, where N is the number of stages.</p><p>Feedback shift registers generate one <span class="spCh spChx2018">&#x2018;</span>progressive<span class="spCh spChx2019">&#x2019;</span> or <span class="spCh spChx2018">&#x2018;</span>creeping<span class="spCh spChx2019">&#x2019;</span> code of length 2N in addition to other codes.
